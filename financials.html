Executive Summary
A scenario-driven view of the Volty U1 business case: what the model is, what it assumes, and where the numbers land. Use the four headline metrics below as the at-a-glance read; every section that follows drills into one driver of these outcomes.

This dashboard is the live financial model behind Volty U1, NUEN MOTO's delivery-optimized, network-agnostic electric utility motorcycle.

Its purpose is to translate a single product strategy (a capital-light, B2B-first fleet play built around cross-compatible battery swapping) into numbers an investor or fleet partner can interrogate. It runs three scenarios (Conservative, Base, Optimistic) across unit volume, growth rate, pricing, and OPEX, and shows the downstream effect on revenue, break-even timing, gross margin, working capital, and investor returns.

The headline read in the Base case: roughly 15,000 units over 24 months, around $22.9M of cumulative revenue, EBITDA turning positive in Q4 2026 and full break-even by month 14, on a margin that arrives with volume: roughly 6% blended at mid-scale, expanding to about 26% blended at full scale. The model is deliberately conservative and explicit about its two pressure points, BOM cost sensitivity and per-unit working capital, both stress-tested in later sections. Every figure on this dashboard reconciles to model v1.6.1; the section footers name the source sheet.

What this means. As scheduled ($250K in April, $550-600K in July, $150-200K in October), the 24-month plan runs to a razor-thin minimum: the Conservative case bottoms at $5,784 of cash in month 11, and the Base and Optimistic cases briefly dip below zero (months 6 and 8) ahead of the October tranche, because faster growth consumes working capital faster. Tranche timing, not total capital, is the binding constraint.

Zero-revenue runway. On the bridge-phase capital of $350K ($250K bridge plus $100K founder capital), at the pre-revenue burn of roughly $40K/month (OPEX plus facility service), the company has about 8 to 9 months of runway before any vehicle revenue. First vehicle cash lands in month 9 (December 2026) in all three scenarios.

How the dips are covered. The $1.0M plan carries about $95K of explicit contingency, and Han Hoang's $100K founder capital sits alongside the bridge, outside the raise, in exactly the window where the dips occur. The alternative is equivalent: release part of the October tranche earlier. Either way, the gap is identified and covered, not discovered later.

Break Even Analysis
When the business stops burning cash, by scenario. Break-even ranges from month 10 (Optimistic, ~3,450 units) to month 18 (Conservative, ~4,325 units), with the Base case at month 14 and ~4,300 units. Units are measured against 24-month fixed costs ($420K tooling, $27K certification, plus cumulative OPEX) at vehicle-only contribution margins of $296 to $371 per unit; counting accessory and service revenue, the all-revenue contribution margin is $503/unit and the hurdle drops to ~2,550 units. The spread is driven almost entirely by how fast monthly volume compounds, shown in the chart on the left and the unit/month detail on the right.

Key Risks & Opportunities
The two things that can break the model, and the demand that can make it. On the left, a BOM cost stress test: run on the model's most exposed basis (Conservative fleet-only pricing, $1,300 against a $1,269 mid-scale all-in cost), BOM is ~93% of unit cost and quotes are firm but not locked, so even a 10% supplier or FX shock flips fleet-only margin negative. That is why supplier price-fixing, local sourcing, and the retail/accessory blend matter; at Base blended pricing the same stresses compress margin from 26% rather than erasing it. On the right, the risk-adjusted customer pipeline: signed and in-progress fleet demand, weighted by close probability.

Closing Statement
The bottom line: why these numbers add up to a business worth backing.

Volty U1 is a deliberately unglamorous bet: a low-margin, high-volume utility vehicle aimed at the part of the market that is actually electrifying first, the fleets.

The model in this dashboard does not rely on a pricing miracle or a premium-brand halo. It works because the unit economics are real at a $1,300 fleet price, because the capital required to reach break-even is measured in hundreds of thousands rather than millions, and because the demand is being pulled forward by regulation rather than marketing. The network-agnostic battery strategy keeps Volty out of the closed-ecosystem trap that constrains its larger competitors, and the capital-light partnership structure means infrastructure cost sits with the battery networks, not with NUEN MOTO.

The honest risks are named here, not hidden: a thin gross margin that a BOM or FX shock can erase, and a per-unit working-capital gap that has to be financed as volume scales. Both are quantified, both have mitigation paths, and both are the right problems to have for a company whose constraint is demand conversion, not demand itself.

The $250K bridge is sized to prove the pilot and convert the signed and in-progress pipeline into deployed units; the $750K seed funds the move from pilot to early production. Everything beyond that is a financing and execution question, not a product question. The product case is made.

NUEN MOTO · Han Hoang, Co-Founder, Head of Investment & Strategy · Confidential
